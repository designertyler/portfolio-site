Consolidated cloud onboarding

Overview

A multi-release initiative to modernize IBM Cloud’s service onboarding by moving off a legacy tool and into a modern, scalable experience. This work unified previously fragmented workflows, improved compliance alignment, and created a path to retire aging infrastructure. This saved the platform an estimated ~$45K per month while resolving long-standing friction points for both service onboarding teams and first-party approvers.

As a Senior UX Designer, I guided the UX for the unified onboarding experience. I documented existing workflows, aligned requirements with SMEs, and designed foundational patterns that include the dashboard, lifecycle approvals, IAM flows, navigation, and information architecture. I collaborated across offering management, research, SMEs, and engineering to ensure functional parity, improved usability, and a clear path to retiring the legacy tool.

The problem

The service onboarding experience in IBM Cloud was split across two separate systems: a legacy tool used for first-party services (RMC) and a more modern tool used for third-party offerings (Partner Center).

These systems act as control planes for bringing offerings into the IBM Cloud Catalog by centralizing onboarding tooling and requirements across billing, IAM, and governance and compliance, and enable publishing products to the marketplace. The separation between the two systems created fragmentation across workflows and limited the platform’s ability to scale and adopt new onboarding models.

"...you may have heard of this acquisition. So, these guys want to onboard into the IBM cloud as a 1st party offering. They saw the service framework, and it has intimidated them to the point of rethinking whether this is a market for them or not.” — Product manager, user-testing participant

Key challenges included:

  • Two different tools and two different mental models for publishing offerings into the IBM Cloud Catalog
  • A legacy system operating on costly, aging infrastructure that was difficult to enhance
  • Limited alignment with current compliance standards and onboarding workflows
  • Minimal visibility for onboarders and approvers into approval status, required actions, or next steps
  • Multiple stakeholder groups (BSS, IAM, security, catalog, ecosystem) each had their own requirements and vocabularies
  • RMC and Partner Center needed to coexist for a period while services migrated in waves
  • Engineering capacity was split across migration work and net-new features such as professional services onboarding

Process

The migration effort had to serve several audiences simultaneously: service owners (1st and 3rd party) onboarding new products, IBM approvers working through Service Framework pillars, ecosystem partners tracking their go-to-market progress, and end users consuming the services. Any change to onboarding flows could impact dozens of teams and millions in marketplace revenue, so we had to evolve the UX carefully with no disruptions.

This project had a large number of stakeholders with their own unique pain points and requirements for sign-off.

Given the number of teams and systems involved, my role focused on driving clarity across the onboarding ecosystem—documenting current workflows, aligning requirements across pillars, and defining the UX patterns needed for a consolidated experience in one tool.

This work took shape through the following activities:

  • Mapping the as-is experience
    Partnered with another designer to document the RMC flows, Service Framework approval journeys, and Partner Center entry points. We used Mural to map end-to-end onboarding journeys for each pillar.

    • Regular collaborative working sessions allowed us to co-create with our architects and deepen our understanding of the system we were designing.

  • Mini-workshops with users
    Helped plan and facilitate a series of as-is journey map sessions led by our researcher to document pain points, required data, and “must-not-break” behaviors.
    • "...when we had to refresh it, I had to scramble to figure out how to do it, and it wasn't intuitive. We also learned it hadn't been maintained. The contact links for the service framework requirements and even the documentation were broken. It was a huge struggle to release anything because so much information was unmaintained.” — Release manager, user-testing participant

    Pain points collected from several workshops and organized along an onboarding journey.

  • Defining migration phases
    With PMs, we framed specific milestones to roll out each capability. Being a part of these conversations early in the planning process ensured the design team had the team we needed to do our best work. Each milestone achieved feature parity in Partner Center for onboarding platform services, then billable services, then professional services, followed by full RMC retirement.

    • I created this Gantt chart to communicate our design process and help estimate a timeline for our stakeholders.

  • Designing core patterns
    Evolved existing key UX patterns such as the onboarding checklist, product dashboard, and navigation to support both first-party and third-party services. We introduced maturity levels to better communicate where a service was in its lifecycle and improved the navigation to be scalable for future enhancements and product types.
    • "So I have to go and check with each pillar, make sure they closed all their issues. ... For simplicity I'd rather get things more streamlined than have all the tools everywhere.” — Pillar approver, user-testing participant

    Users consistently struggled to understand their approval status, maturity level, remaining work, and updated documentation. The updated checklist and improved pillar-approval presentation resolved these issues with clearer system status and in-context links to the latest documentation.

  • Hi-fi prototypes and user testing
    Created Figma prototypes for testing scenarios (new service onboarding, existing RMC-based service migration, lifecycle updates) and validated them with users from our onboarder, approver, and development team personas.
    • “Based on the amount of time that I spent having to report from our Git repo, to leadership, to the team, and the chasing around, I think what has been proposed so far will be a huge impact to the reduction of time.” — Release manager, user-testing participant

    Usability testing was conducted in several rounds with painpoints and opportunities being tracked as issues in our team's GitHub repo.

  • Iterating from feedback and new feature releases
    As implementation progressed, we refined copy, state handling, edge cases, and itterated based on user feedback. Once RMC was retired, the team was able to shift to delivering net-new features.

    • The updated codebase allowed us to quickly ship small usability improvements. For example, fixing an issue where users couldn’t remove a role if it was the only role for an action would have previously required a prohibitively time-intensive change.

    Outcomes

    • New platform services now onboard through Partner Center only.
      New first-party platform services no longer touch RMC, reducing cognitive load for product teams and concentrating enhancements in one tool.
    • Retired RMC and legacy infrastructure with no service disruption.
      With migration flows in place, the organization now has a runway to retire the last remaining legacy workloads, unlocking approximately $45,000 per month in infrastructure savings.
    • Improved visibility into onboarding status.
      Updated dashboards and checklist patterns give service owners and approvers a shared view of progress, outstanding tasks, and compliance posture.
    • Modern UI.
      The interface was updated to align with the latest IBM design system and brand guidelines, transforming the legacy tool’s functionality into a modern, responsive experience.
    • Reduce onboarding time and frustration for product teams.
      We took this opportunity to fight for the user’s voice by not only meeting the technical requirements, but also addressing long standing pain points. This resulted in more automation, clearer UI, and positive user feedback.
    • Enable future enhancements.
      The redesign was built with scalability and modernization in mind. This made future enhancements easier and faster to deliver.
      • "But I've got to tell you, at a baseline what we are seeing here...
      I think it is truly a quantum leap.” — Product manager, user-testing participant
